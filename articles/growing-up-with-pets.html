The Emotional Benefits of Growing Up with Pets

📅 June 2, 2026📖 8 min read❤️ Family

I grew up with a golden retriever named Max. He arrived when I was six years old, a clumsy bundle of fur who followed me everywhere. He was my confidant, my playmate, and sometimes my accomplice in minor childhood mischief. When I had a bad day at school, Max didn't need me to explain — he'd rest his head on my knee and look at me with those big brown eyes, and somehow everything felt a little more manageable.

Looking back, I realize Max taught me things that no human teacher could. He taught me about unconditional love — the kind that doesn't depend on grades, performance, or being on your best behavior. He taught me about responsibility, because he depended on me for food, walks, and care. And he taught me about loss, because he eventually grew old and left, and that was the first time I truly grieved. I wouldn't trade those lessons for anything.

What the Research Says

The science backs up what many of us have felt intuitively. A 2017 study published in the Journal of Pediatric Nursing found that children who grow up with pets show higher levels of empathy and self-esteem compared to children who don't. Another study from the University of Cambridge (2020) tracked 2,000 families and found that children with dogs had significantly fewer behavioral problems and better emotional regulation than their peers without pets.

But here's what I find most interesting: the research suggests that the type of relationship matters more than the type of pet. A child who actively participates in caring for a hamster may derive as much benefit as a child who plays fetch with a dog every day. The key is the connection, the responsibility, and the mutual trust.

Empathy: The Hidden Curriculum

When a child learns to recognize that a cat's flattened ears mean "I'm scared" or that a dog's tucked tail means "I'm nervous," they're building an emotional vocabulary that transfers directly to human relationships. I've seen it in my own niece and nephew. Since adopting a rescue cat named Miso, my nephew has become remarkably attuned to other people's feelings. He notices when someone is upset. He asks, "Are you okay?" He learned that from watching Miso.

Some parents worry that pets might be too much responsibility for children. And it's true that the bulk of pet care should fall on adults. But even small tasks — filling the water bowl, brushing the dog, choosing which toy to play with — give children a sense of purpose and competence that builds confidence.

❤️ A Personal Note: I believe the greatest gift a pet gives a child is a judgment-free relationship. In a world where children are constantly evaluated, ranked, and critiqued, a pet offers pure acceptance. Your dog doesn't care if you got a B instead of an A. Your cat doesn't care if you struck out at your baseball game. That kind of unconditional acceptance is rare and precious.

The Controversial Side: Pets and Allergies

There's a long-running debate about whether early exposure to pets increases or decreases allergy risk. Earlier generations of doctors sometimes advised families to rehome pets when a child developed allergies. The more current thinking, supported by studies from the National Institutes of Health, is that early exposure to dogs and cats may actually reduce the risk of developing certain allergies and asthma. A 2018 study found that children in households with dogs had a 13% lower risk of developing asthma compared to those without.

That said, every child is different, and severe allergies are a real concern. If your child has a known allergy, talk to an allergist before bringing a pet home. Some families find that hypoallergenic breeds or pets like fish and reptiles are a better fit.

Pets and Mental Health in Adolescence

The teenage years can be brutal. Between academic pressure, social dynamics, and the general emotional turmoil of adolescence, having a pet can be an incredible anchor. Several studies have shown that teenagers who have strong bonds with their pets report lower levels of stress and anxiety. There's something about talking to a dog who never interrupts, never judges, and never repeats your secrets to the wrong person.

I interviewed a high school counselor once who told me that she often suggested pets as a coping strategy for anxious students — and not just as a casual suggestion. She'd actually work with families to find appropriate pets for teens struggling with anxiety or depression. She said the routine of caring for another living being — the structure, the purpose, the physical contact — was sometimes more effective than any other intervention she could recommend.

Grief and Loss: The Hardest Lesson

No article about growing up with pets would be honest without addressing the inevitable goodbye. The lifespan of a dog or cat is tragically shorter than a human's, and losing a childhood pet is often a child's first experience with death. It's heartbreaking. But it's also, in my opinion, an essential part of growing up.

Learning to grieve, to remember, and to heal is a fundamental human skill. A child who loses a pet learns that grief is survivable. That the sadness eventually softens. That it's okay to cry, and it's okay to laugh at happy memories. These are lessons that serve them well throughout life. I still think about Max, thirty years later. The grief is long gone. But the gratitude remains.

Final Thoughts

If you're considering whether to get a pet for your family, my advice is simple: don't think of it as buying a pet for your children. Think of it as inviting a new member into your family — one who will teach, comfort, challenge, and ultimately enrich your children's lives in ways you can't yet imagine. It won't always be easy. There will be chewed shoes and scratched furniture and early morning walks in the rain. But the return on that investment, measured in love and lessons learned, is incalculable.

Max has been gone for many years, but I still carry him with me. Every time I see a golden retriever wagging its tail, I smile. Every time I comfort a friend who's lost a pet, I draw on what Max taught me. That's the legacy of growing up with a pet — not just the memories, but the person you become. ❤️🐾
