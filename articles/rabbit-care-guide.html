Rabbits Aren't 'Easy Pets' — The Complete Care Guide Every New Owner Needs

📅 June 2, 2026📖 6 min read🐰 Small Pets

"Rabbits are great starter pets for kids." I hear that sentence at pet stores, from well-meaning relatives, and even from some breeders. It's the single biggest lie in the pet industry, and it's the reason thousands of rabbits end up in shelters every single year.

I've owned rabbits for over a decade. I've nursed them through medical emergencies, watched them binky across my living room at 3 AM, and cried my eyes out when I lost one. Here's the truth: rabbits are not easy pets. They're expensive, fragile, socially complex, and require as much commitment as a dog or cat. But if you get it right — if you actually meet their needs — they're some of the most rewarding animals you'll ever share your home with.

This isn't a fluffy overview. This is the real guide, with real numbers, real costs, and the hard truths no one tells you at the pet store checkout counter.

The Housing Lie That Needs to Die

That wire-bottomed hutch at the pet store? The one with the little water bottle strapped to the side? It's a cage, not a home. Rabbits need space to run, jump, stretch to full height on their hind legs, and do zoomies. A hutch doesn't provide any of that.

The minimum living space for a single rabbit is 32 square feet. That's an 8-foot by 4-foot x-pen. For two bonded rabbits, you want even more. And that's just their enclosure — they also need 4+ hours of supervised free-roam time every single day.

My setup: a 48-inch x-pen in my living room, lined with a low-pile rug (rabbits hate sliding on hard floors), connected to a hidey-house and a litter box. The pen gate stays open during the day so my rabbits free-roam the room. I've rabbit-proofed everything — electrical cords in spiral tubing, baseboards covered with plastic corner guards, toxic houseplants removed entirely.

Outdoor hutches are worse. Rabbits living outside face predators (raccoons, hawks, neighborhood dogs), extreme temperatures, and chronic loneliness. They die younger. They're more stressed. Please, keep your rabbit indoors. They're not lawn ornaments.

Diet: 80-10-5, No Exceptions

Rabbits have a digestive system so delicate it stops working if they go more than 12 hours without eating. Their diet breaks down like this:

  • 80% unlimited timothy hay (or orchard grass, meadow hay, oat hay — not alfalfa, which is too rich for adult rabbits)
  • 10% fresh leafy greens
  • 5% high-fiber pellets (timothy-based, no seeds or colorful bits)
  • 5% treats (small amounts of fruit or herbs)

Hay is not optional. It's not a side dish. Hay is 80% of what your rabbit should eat, period. Hay wears down their constantly-growing teeth, keeps their gut moving, and prevents obesity. If your rabbit isn't eating hay, something is wrong — either the hay is low-quality, your rabbit has dental pain, or they're being overfed pellets.

Safe vegetables (daily): romaine lettuce, red leaf lettuce, green leaf lettuce, cilantro, basil, dill, mint, parsley, kale (small amounts), carrot tops (not the carrots themselves), bell peppers, bok choy, arugula, watercress.

Unsafe or limit: iceberg lettuce (zero nutrition), spinach and Swiss chard (high oxalates — small amounts only), broccoli and cauliflower (can cause gas), carrots and fruit (treat only — high sugar), beans, grains, nuts, seeds, corn, yogurt drops, any human junk food.

Pellets should be measured, not unlimited. A 5-pound rabbit gets about 1/4 cup of pellets per day. Overfeeding pellets is the #1 cause of obesity and selective eating (they fill up on pellets and ignore hay, which leads to dental disease and GI stasis).

What Your Rabbit's Behavior Actually Means

Rabbits are not "quiet pets." They're just quiet in ways you have to learn to read. Here's their language:

  • Binky — that crazy jump with a mid-air twist and a kick. Pure happiness. If your rabbit binkies, you're doing something right.
  • Tooth purring (bruxing) — a soft, gentle grinding sound. This means contentment. They're basically purring.
  • Loud tooth grinding — this is NOT purring. This means pain. If you hear a harsh, grating grind, go to the vet immediately.
  • Thumping — one sharp stomp of the back foot. It means "danger" or "I'm annoyed." My rabbit thumps when the neighbor's cat walks past the window.
  • Flopping — they dramatically throw themselves onto their side. Looks alarming. Actually means "I feel so safe I'm going to pass out right here."
  • Circling your feet — mating behavior. Common in unspayed/unneutered rabbits. Also sometimes just excitement.
  • Chinning — rubbing their chin on objects. They have scent glands there. They're marking everything as theirs.

Every rabbit has a distinct personality. I've had a shy, introverted rabbit who hid from everyone, and a loud, demanding rabbit who would nudge my ankle until I fed her. They're as individual as dogs. Get to know yours.

GI Stasis: The Emergency You Need to Know About

I'll never forget the call from my friend Sarah at 11 PM. Her 3-year-old rabbit, Mochi, had stopped eating. Just stopped. No interest in her favorite cilantro. No droppings in her litter box.

I told Sarah to get to the emergency vet immediately. GI stasis — where the digestive system literally stops moving — can kill a rabbit in 24 to 48 hours. The vet bill came to just over $2,000 for overnight hospitalization, fluids, motility drugs, syringe feeding, and pain medication. Mochi was touch-and-go for three days. She survived, but barely. Sarah now has an emergency fund specifically for her rabbits.

Signs of GI stasis: no droppings for 8-12 hours, refusing food (especially hay and favorite treats), hunched posture, teeth grinding (the painful kind), bloated belly, lethargy.

What to do: Call a rabbit-savvy vet immediately. Do NOT wait overnight. Do NOT try home remedies. Do NOT give simethicone and hope it passes. This is a genuine life-threatening emergency. Find a vet before you need one — not when your rabbit is dying at 2 AM.

Social Needs: Your Rabbit Needs a Rabbit

Here's a hard truth: you cannot replace a rabbit companion. Rabbits are herd animals. They evolved to live in large social groups. A single rabbit, even one with a devoted human, can suffer from depression, loneliness, and stress-related illness.

The ideal setup is a bonded pair. A neutered male and spayed female is the most successful combination. Bonding rabbits takes time — you're essentially introducing two strangers who might fight, and gradually getting them to tolerate, then groom, then love each other. It can take weeks or months. You'll need neutral territory, patience, and a lot of stress balls.

But watching two bonded rabbits groom each other, flop side by side, and share a pile of hay? It's worth every difficult day of the bonding process.

If you can only have one rabbit (and sometimes that's the reality), you need to be prepared to spend several hours daily interacting with them. Not just sitting in the same room, but actually engaging — sitting on the floor at their level, letting them climb on you, offering treats, talking to them.

The Real Cost of Rabbit Ownership

Let's talk money, because no one does.

Upfront costs ($300-500):

  • X-pen or enclosure: $60-120
  • Litter box and supplies: $30-50
  • Hay feeder, water bowl (not bottle), food bowls: $20-40
  • Spay/neuter surgery: $150-500 (yes, this is necessary)
  • Initial vet checkup: $50-100
  • Rabbit-proofing supplies: $30-60
  • Carrier: $30-60

Monthly costs ($60-100):

  • Timothy hay (the good stuff from Small Pet Select or Oxbow): $25-40
  • Fresh vegetables: $15-25
  • Pellets: $10-15
  • Litter (paper-based, not clay or clumping): $10-20
  • Toys and enrichment: $10-20

Yearly vet costs ($200-500+):

  • Annual checkup: $50-100
  • Vaccinations (depending on region): $50-100
  • Nail trims: $10-20 per session
  • Emergency fund: set aside at least $500-1,000

Rabbits live 8 to 12 years. That's over $10,000 in total ownership costs for a pair. If that number makes you uncomfortable, a rabbit might not be the right pet for you right now — and that's okay. Better to know now than to surrender a rabbit because you can't afford it.

Three Mistakes That Kill Rabbits

1. Picking them up incorrectly. Rabbits have fragile skeletons — their spines make up about 8% of their body weight. If they kick out while being held without proper hind-leg support, they can fracture their back. The right way: one hand under the chest, one hand supporting the hindquarters, held securely against your body. Many rabbits never learn to enjoy being picked up. That's fine. Sit on the floor with them instead.

2. Ignoring GI stasis. I already covered this. It deserves repeating. "They'll eat when they're hungry" — no, they won't. Their gut physically stops moving. They need a vet. Every hour matters.

3. Keeping a rabbit alone. I've seen so many "single" rabbits develop behavioral problems — aggression, depression, excessive chewing. Rabbits are not solitary animals. If you can't commit to a pair, get two anyway. They'll keep each other company while you're at work.

Are You Actually Ready for a Rabbit?

Rabbits are not a trial run before you get a dog. They're not a "gentle starter pet" for kids. They're exotic animals with specific medical needs, fragile bodies, and complex social requirements. A rabbit that lives 10 years will go through phases — the terrible teenage phase (4-6 months with hormones raging), the grumpy senior years, the health scares that cost thousands of dollars.

But they'll also greet you at the gate every morning, circle your feet when they want attention, flop next to you on the couch, and binky across the room because they're genuinely happy to be alive.

If you've read this far and you're still in: find a rabbit-savvy vet first, set up a proper x-pen, buy a bale of timothy hay, and adopt a bonded pair from a rescue. Don't buy from a pet store. Don't buy from a breeder. There are thousands of rabbits waiting in shelters for someone who actually understands what they're signing up for.

They're not easy pets. But they're worth it.
