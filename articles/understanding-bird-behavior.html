What Your Bird Is Trying to Tell You — A Behavior Guide for Parrot Parents

📅 May 25, 2026 📖 6 min read 🐦 Birds

I'll never forget the first time Mango bit me. One second he was happily perched on my finger, the next those beak clamps sank into my knuckle like he was testing a walnut for freshness. I yelped, he flew off, and I sat there bleeding and bewildered. What did I do wrong?

Turns out, I just didn't speak bird.

I've owned parrots for over a decade now, and I've made just about every mistake you can make. The good news? Birds are incredibly honest creatures. They tell you exactly how they feel — you just have to learn the language. Let me walk you through what I've picked up, the hard way, so maybe you can skip some of the scars.

The Eyes Have It — Reading Bird Body Language

If you only learn one thing about bird body language, make it this: watch the eyes. When a parrot's pupils rapidly dilate and contract — called "pinning" or "flashing" — something big is happening in that little brain. Excitement can cause pinning. So can fear. So can "I'm about to bite your face off." The context tells you which one it is.

Here are the signals I check for every single day with my birds:

  • Eye pinning — Rapid pupil changes. Paired with leaned-forward posture and sleeked feathers? Your bird is amped up, and not necessarily in a good way. Back off. Paired with happy chattering and relaxed feathers? They're excited to see you.
  • Wing quivering — A little flutter, almost like a shiver. I see this when my conure is deciding whether to fly to me or not. It's excitement mixed with indecision.
  • Tail bobbing — A gentle up-and-down with each breath can just mean your bird is breathing hard after exercise. But if they're sitting still and the tail is pumping noticeably, that's a vet visit. Respiratory trouble is no joke in birds.
  • Fluffed feathers — Puffing up can mean "I'm cozy and ready for a nap." But a bird that stays fluffed all day, with droopy posture and closed eyes? That's a sick bird.
  • One foot up — Classic relaxed pose. If your bird is standing on one leg with the other tucked into their belly fluff, they feel safe. Congratulations.
  • Beak grinding — That soft grinding sound before sleep. It's the bird equivalent of a cat purring. Pure contentment.

Decoding the Noise

Birds are loud. It's one of the first things new owners realize, usually around 6 AM on a Saturday. But not all noise is created equal.

Contact calls are those repetitive, piercing squawks your bird makes when you leave the room. In the wild, flock members call to keep track of each other. Your bird isn't trying to annoy you — they're saying "Hey, where'd you go? Are you okay?" The fix is simple: call back. A cheerful "I'm right here!" from the other room is often enough to settle them down.

Alarm calls are sharp, urgent, and totally different from the usual chatter. When my cockatiel spots a hawk through the window, she lets out a specific screech that I've learned means "danger from above." I thank her, close the curtain, and she quiets down immediately. She did her job.

Contentment sounds — soft chattering, singing, mimicry, tongue-clicking. This is your bird in their happy place. My Senegal parrot talks to his toys when he's having a good day. It's adorable and I absolutely eavesdrop.

Then there's the flock call. This is the big one — a loud, sustained scream that carries. Wild parrots do it at dawn and dusk to check in with the flock. Your pet parrot still has that instinct. If your bird screams every morning at sunrise and every evening at sunset, congrats — you own a parrot. It's normal. Work around it.

Three Common Problems (And What They Really Mean)

Screaming That Won't Stop

If your bird screams nonstop, all day, every day, something is wrong. Parrots in good environments are loud sometimes, but not constantly. The root cause is almost always boredom, loneliness, or lack of foraging opportunities.

I see owners who respond to screaming by rushing over and saying "Shh, it's okay." Congratulations — you just trained your bird that screaming gets your attention. Now you have a screamer for life. Instead, ask yourself: does my bird have enough toys? Enough out-of-cage time? Enough mental stimulation? A foraging toy that takes fifteen minutes to crack open is worth an hour of peace.

Feather Plucking

This one breaks my heart every time I see it. A bird that plucks its own feathers — ripping them out one by one — is a bird in serious distress. It could be medical (skin infections, allergies, pain), but more often it's psychological. Boredom, loneliness, hormonal frustration, or a sudden change in routine can trigger plucking.

I knew a cockatiel named Kiwi who started plucking his chest feathers bald after his owner switched from working nights to days. The bird's entire schedule flipped, and nobody explained it to him. He just knew his person left at the wrong time and came home at the wrong time, and his little bird brain couldn't cope. It took months of extra attention, new toys, and a consistent bedtime routine to get his feathers growing back.

Once plucking becomes a habit, it's incredibly hard to stop. Prevention is everything. Keep their environment stable, keep them busy, and watch for the first signs of over-preening.

Biting

Here's the truth: birds bite for a reason. They don't bite "for no reason" — you just missed the reason.

A bite is a last resort. Before it happens, your bird will give you warning signs. Eye pinning. Leaning away. Pinning ears flat against the head. Tail fanning. If you ignore all of those, the beak comes next. Don't punish a bird for biting. They're communicating the only way they have left. Punishment just teaches them that you're unpredictable and scary, which makes future bites more likely.

Instead, figure out what you did wrong. Were you asking them to step up when they didn't want to? Did you reach into the cage too fast? Are they hormonal and territorial? Apologize (yes, out loud), give them space, and do better next time.

How I Train (No Force, No Fear)

I use positive reinforcement, full stop. No grabbing, no scruffing, no "dominance." Birds aren't dogs. You can't alpha-roll a parrot.

Here's my approach: I carry a little cup of safflower seeds — my birds would sell me out for a single safflower. When my conure does something I want, like stepping up calmly or letting me touch his foot without biting, he gets a seed and a happy "Good boy!" I never force him to do anything. I just make the right choice the most rewarding choice.

Does it take patience? Yes. My Senegal took six months to voluntarily step onto my hand. But now he does it every time, with zero stress. That relationship is built on trust, not fear.

Positive reinforcement works for everything. Want your bird to stop screaming? Reward quiet moments. Want them to try a new vegetable? Put a tiny piece in their bowl and celebrate when they touch it. Break it down into baby steps and be consistent.

Getting the Essentials Right

A bird's cage should feel like a playground, not a prison. I aim for at least 4 to 6 toys in the cage at any time, and I rotate them weekly so nothing gets stale. Foraging toys — the kind that hide treats inside — are worth their weight in gold. A bird that spends twenty minutes working a puzzle to get a sunflower seed is a bird that isn't screaming or plucking.

Out-of-cage time matters just as much. I give my birds at least 2 to 3 hours outside the cage every day. That's non-negotiable. A parrot that lives in a cage 23 hours a day will develop behavioral problems, period.

Don't Ignore the Screaming, Don't Punish the Bite

These are the two biggest mistakes I see new bird owners make, so I'll say them again separately.

Ignoring screaming doesn't fix it. If your bird is screaming because they're bored, ignoring them just teaches them you're not coming. The screaming escalates. By the time you finally crack and go see what's wrong, you've reinforced the loudest, longest scream. The solution is to figure out why they're screaming and address the cause — not the symptom.

Punishing a bite erodes trust. If you yell, flick, or blow on your bird's face after a bite, you've just taught them that hands are dangerous. Next time, they'll bite harder and faster because they expect punishment. The better move: don't react. Set the bird down calmly, walk away, and think about what signal you missed.

A Few More Things I Wish Someone Had Told Me

  • Birds are messy. Like, impressively messy. They throw food, they bathe in their water bowl, and they produce a truly astonishing amount of dust. Accept this early.
  • They're also stubborn. A parrot who doesn't want to go back in the cage will not go back in the cage until they decide it's time. You can't rush a bird.
  • Hormones are a whole season. Spring hits and your sweet cuddlebug might turn into a territorial dinosaur. It passes. Don't take it personally.
  • Talk to your bird constantly. Narrate what you're doing. They're learning your language even if you don't realize it. My conure says "Good morning" on weekdays but not weekends because he figured out the pattern.

The more time I spend with birds, the more I realize they're not trying to give us a hard time. They're having a hard time, and they're telling us about it the only way they know how. Every scream, every bite, every feather fluff is a message. Our job is to learn to read it.

Happy bird keeping, and may your fingers stay bite-free.
